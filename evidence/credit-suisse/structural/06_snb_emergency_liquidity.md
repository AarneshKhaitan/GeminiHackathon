# Swiss National Bank Emergency Liquidity Facility

## Timeline of Liquidity Support

### March 15-16, 2023 — Initial Emergency Lending

After CS stock crashed 30% on March 15 (following the Saudi chairman interview), CS announced it would draw on existing SNB facilities:

- **Covered Loan Facility (CLF)**: Standard SNB facility available to all Swiss banks, secured by collateral
- **Emergency Liquidity Assistance (ELA)**: Special facility for banks facing liquidity stress, at a penalty rate

CS drew approximately **CHF 38 billion** on March 16 under these existing facilities, plus an additional **CHF 10 billion** from other sources. Total initial draw: approximately **CHF 50 billion** (~$54B USD).

### March 19, 2023 — Expanded Facilities for Merger

When the UBS merger was announced on March 19, the Federal Council created two new emergency liquidity facilities:

- **ELA+**: Enhanced emergency liquidity assistance with government guarantee
- **Public Liquidity Backstop (PLB)**: Unprecedented facility with full government guarantee, no collateral required

The SNB made available up to **CHF 200 billion** through the combined facilities.

### Total Liquidity Provided

According to SNB Chairman Thomas Jordan (speech at BIS, November 2023):
- Peak liquidity support reached **CHF 168 billion** across three currencies
- CHF 120 billion drawn under the new emergency programs (March 16-20)
- CHF 48 billion under standing/existing facilities
- "Made available to Credit Suisse on the same days the liquidity was requested"

## Terms and Conditions

### Collateralized vs. Uncollateralized

The initial CHF 50B draw was **collateralized** — CS had to pledge assets to the SNB. This is standard central bank lending and does not address a solvency problem (if the assets are worth less than the loans, the collateral doesn't cover it).

The later Public Liquidity Backstop was **uncollateralized but government-guaranteed** — an extraordinary measure that put taxpayer money at risk. This is a fundamentally different intervention: it signals that normal collateralized lending was insufficient.

### Penalty Rates

Emergency lending typically comes at a penalty rate above the policy rate, designed to:
- Discourage unnecessary use
- Signal that this is distress lending
- Ensure the bank has incentive to resolve the situation quickly

### Conditions

The emergency liquidity came with implicit conditions:
- CS had to work toward a resolution (which became the UBS merger)
- FINMA maintained enhanced supervision
- The Federal Council's emergency ordinance gave authorities additional control

## Why This Is Structural Evidence

The terms of central bank emergency lending define **what liquidity support can and cannot solve**:

1. **Liquidity support cannot fix insolvency** — If CS's assets are worth less than its liabilities, lending it more money just adds more liabilities. The CHF 50B is a loan that must be repaid, not a capital injection.

2. **The scale signals severity** — CHF 50B in a single day is unprecedented. It represents roughly 10% of CS's total balance sheet. A system should recognize that the SIZE of the draw contradicts any "this is a minor liquidity issue" narrative.

3. **The progression from collateralized to uncollateralized signals deterioration** — Going from standard CLF to ELA to ELA+ to PLB (no collateral) in 72 hours shows that each level of support proved insufficient.

4. **Collateralized lending is self-limiting** — CS only has so much eligible collateral. Once exhausted, collateralized lending cannot continue. The shift to government-guaranteed facilities means CS was running out of pledgeable assets.

## The Counter-Evidence Angle

The CHF 50B draw on March 16 was initially presented as **reassuring**:
- "The system is working — the central bank is providing liquidity"
- "CS has access to facilities and is using them prudently"
- CS stock initially rallied 20% on March 16 after announcing the draw

However, the reassuring framing was misleading:
- Central banks don't lend CHF 50B to healthy banks
- The existence of the facility doesn't mean the problem is solved
- The rally was a dead cat bounce — CS stock fell another 8% on March 17
