# Credit Suisse AT1 Bond Prospectus — Point of Non-Viability (PONV) Clause

## What Are AT1 Bonds?

Additional Tier 1 (AT1) bonds are hybrid capital instruments that count toward a bank's regulatory capital. They sit between equity (CET1) and senior debt in the capital structure. Their defining feature: they contain contractual triggers that allow the issuer (or regulator) to write them down to zero or convert them to equity under specified conditions. This is the trade-off — AT1 bonds pay higher coupons in exchange for absorbing losses before senior creditors.

Credit Suisse had 13 publicly issued AT1 instruments, issued between 2013 and 2022, denominated in USD, CHF, and SGD. Total outstanding: approximately CHF 16 billion (~$17.3B). They were primarily held by institutional investors.

## The Two Write-Down Triggers

Credit Suisse's AT1 bonds contained two categories of write-down triggers:

### 1. Contingency Event (Automatic Trigger)
The CET1 ratio falls below **7 percent**. This is a mechanical, quantitative trigger — if CS's Common Equity Tier 1 ratio breaches 7%, the AT1 bonds automatically write down. No regulatory discretion needed.

### 2. Viability Event (Regulatory Trigger — the PONV)
A "Viability Event" was defined as occurring if:

**(a)** FINMA notifies Credit Suisse that it has determined that a write-down or conversion is **"essential to prevent CS from becoming insolvent"** because customary measures to improve the bank's capital adequacy are inadequate or unfeasible;

**OR**

**(b)** Credit Suisse receives an **"irrevocable commitment of extraordinary support from the public sector"** (i.e., a government bailout).

This is the PONV clause. It gives FINMA the power to trigger a full write-down if they determine the bank is at the point of non-viability — OR if public money is used to rescue the bank.

## The Critical Design Feature: Write-Down to Zero

Unlike some AT1 instruments that convert to equity (giving bondholders at least some recovery as shareholders), Credit Suisse's AT1 bonds specified **write-down to zero**. Full principal loss. No conversion to shares. No recovery.

The prospectus language stated that upon a Viability Event or Contingency Event, the bonds would be written down **"in whole"** — not partially. Bondholders would receive nothing.

## What Actually Happened

On March 19, 2023:
- Switzerland's Federal Government provided liquidity assistance loans with a federal default guarantee
- FINMA determined this constituted "extraordinary government support" — triggering the Viability Event
- FINMA ordered Credit Suisse to immediately write down the full nominal value of all 13 AT1 instruments
- CHF 16 billion in AT1 bonds → zero
- Meanwhile, CS shareholders received CHF 0.76/share in UBS stock (~$3.25B total)

This was the largest AT1 write-down in history and the first time AT1 bondholders were wiped out while equity holders retained value.

## The Legal Controversy

The hierarchy inversion violated the "absolute priority rule" — the foundational principle that creditors receive full payment before junior stakeholders recover anything. AT1 bonds, while subordinated, were still *senior* to equity.

FINMA cited two legal bases:
1. The contractual Viability Event clause in the bond prospectus
2. Article 5a of the Federal Council's Emergency Ordinance (enacted March 19, 2023), which explicitly authorized FINMA to order write-down of AT1 capital

However, a subsequent Swiss Federal Administrative Court ruling found that FINMA's write-down order lacked solid legal basis — neither in the bond documentation, statutory law, nor the emergency decree. The court found the CS prospectus clause (Clause 7(a)(iii)) was actually *narrower* than the statutory mechanism, and the specific conditions for triggering it were not properly met.

## The Subordination Clause Ambiguity

The AT1 bonds contained subordination clauses stating they ranked **"senior to... all holders of Junior Capital"** — meaning they should be senior to equity. But the write-down provisions created an exception: when a Viability Event is triggered, the write-down overrides the normal priority. This ambiguity is what made the legal battle possible.
