# Saudi Ownership Cap — The Structural Constraint

## The Situation

Saudi National Bank (SNB) became Credit Suisse's largest shareholder in November 2022, acquiring a 9.9% stake for approximately CHF 1.4 billion ($1.5B). This investment was part of CS's CHF 4 billion capital raise to fund its restructuring plan.

At the time of purchase, SNB's chairman described CS shares as "a steal" at the entry price.

## The Regulatory Constraint

The 9.9% stake was not arbitrary. It sat precisely at the maximum threshold below which multiple regulatory triggers are avoided:

1. **SAMA (Saudi Arabian Monetary Authority)**: Saudi banking regulations impose enhanced scrutiny and approval requirements for ownership stakes above 10% in foreign financial institutions.

2. **Swiss regulations**: Under Swiss law, ownership above 10% triggers additional regulatory notifications and potential conditions.

3. **EU/EEA regulations**: For CS's European operations, ownership above 10% triggers the "qualifying holding" provisions, requiring regulatory approval.

As SNB Chairman Ammar Al Khudairy explained on Bloomberg TV (March 15, 2023): "If we go above 10%, all kinds of new rules kick in, whether be it by our regulator or the European regulator or the Swiss regulator."

## Why This Eliminates a Hypothesis

Before Al Khudairy's interview, the market was pricing in some probability of "Saudi rescue" — the hypothesis that SNB, as the largest shareholder, would increase its stake to provide more capital to CS. This is a natural hypothesis: the biggest shareholder has the most to lose from CS's failure, so they might throw good money after bad.

The 9.9% cap **structurally eliminates this hypothesis**. It's not that SNB *chose* not to invest more — they *could not* without triggering a multi-jurisdictional regulatory process that would take months, not days. During an acute crisis weekend, this path is simply unavailable.

## The March 15 Interview — The Most Important Cross-Modal Moment

On March 15, 2023, Bloomberg TV interviewed Al Khudairy. When asked if SNB would increase its stake in CS, he replied:

**"Absolutely not, for many reasons beyond the simplest reason, which is regulatory and statutory."**

His statement was:
- **Factually true**: The regulatory constraint was real
- **Reasonable**: No rational actor would take on additional regulatory burden during a crisis
- **Catastrophic in effect**: CS stock dropped **30% intraday** after the interview

The market heard "our biggest shareholder won't save us" — even though Al Khudairy was simply stating a structural fact. The system needs to process BOTH the structural truth AND the market's interpretation.

## The Aftermath

- CS stock fell to CHF 1.55 (from ~CHF 2.20 before the interview) — a 30% single-day crash
- Al Khudairy resigned as SNB chairman on March 27, 2023 — two weeks after the interview
- SNB's entire CHF 1.4B investment was reduced to approximately CHF 280M when the UBS merger was announced at CHF 0.76/share — a loss of over $1 billion
- SNB later said the panic was "unwarranted" and that they had confidence in CS's restructuring (counter-evidence that came too late)
