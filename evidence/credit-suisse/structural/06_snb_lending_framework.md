# SNB Emergency Lending Framework

The Swiss National Bank (SNB) serves as lender of last resort for Swiss banks under a framework established by the National Bank Act and the Banking Act.

---

## Legal Basis

- **National Bank Act (Art. 5(2)(e))**: SNB shall "contribute to the stability of the financial system"
- **National Bank Act (Art. 9)**: SNB may "grant credit to banks and other financial market participants against adequate collateral" — but only for monetary policy purposes
- **Banking Act (Art. 30)**: In a restructuring, FINMA can request SNB to provide liquidity support

## Standard Emergency Liquidity Assistance (ELA)

Under the existing pre-crisis framework:
- SNB provides **collateralized** lending only — the borrower must pledge assets
- Eligible collateral includes: mortgage portfolios, government bonds, investment-grade corporate bonds
- The SNB does NOT accept unsecured lending to banks
- ELA is at the SNB's discretion — banks have no automatic right to it
- ELA carries a penalty rate above the market rate

## Key Structural Limitation

The SNB's pre-crisis framework had a critical constraint: **no uncollateralized lending**. If a bank exhausted its eligible collateral, the SNB could not lend further without a change in law.

This is exactly what happened with Credit Suisse: the CHF 50 billion ELA facility (March 15, 2023) was exhausted within days, requiring the Emergency Ordinance to authorize an additional CHF 100 billion in **federally guaranteed** liquidity — a mechanism that did not exist in the pre-crisis framework.

## Comparison: ECB vs Fed vs SNB Emergency Lending

| Feature | ECB | Fed (Discount Window) | SNB |
|---------|-----|----------------------|-----|
| Collateral required | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Government guarantee available | Via national central banks | Yes (13(3) facilities) | Only via Emergency Ordinance |
| Deposit insurance backing | Yes (EDIS proposal) | Yes (FDIC, $250K) | Partial (Esisuisse, CHF 100K, industry-funded) |
| Lender of last resort explicit | Yes | Yes | Implicit only |

## Structural Implication

The SNB's inability to lend without collateral (absent emergency legislation) meant that once CS exhausted its pledge-able assets, the only remaining options were:
1. Emergency federal guarantee (requires Federal Council action)
2. Forced merger
3. Controlled bankruptcy

This structural constraint shaped the crisis timeline.
