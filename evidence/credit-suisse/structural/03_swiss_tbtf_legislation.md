# Swiss "Too Big To Fail" (TBTF) Legislation

## Overview

Switzerland enacted its TBTF framework after the 2008 financial crisis (specifically after the government-assisted rescue of UBS in 2008). The framework applies to systemically important banks — at the time of the Credit Suisse crisis, this meant Credit Suisse and UBS (the only two Swiss G-SIBs).

## Key Components

### Designation
Under Swiss law, FINMA designates banks as "systemically important" based on:
- Size (total assets, domestic market share)
- Interconnectedness with the financial system
- Substitutability of services provided
- Complexity of operations

Credit Suisse and UBS were both designated as G-SIBs (Global Systemically Important Banks), subject to the highest tier of requirements.

### Enhanced Capital Requirements
Systemically important banks must hold:
- Higher CET1 ratios than non-systemic banks
- Additional "gone concern" capital (AT1 and Tier 2 instruments designed to absorb losses in resolution)
- Total loss-absorbing capacity (TLAC) meeting FSB standards
- Credit Suisse's total capital requirement was approximately 28.6% of risk-weighted assets

### Resolution Planning
Each systemically important bank must maintain:
- A recovery plan (what the bank does to save itself)
- A resolution plan (what authorities do if recovery fails)
- Demonstration of "resolvability" — that critical functions can continue even if the bank fails

### Resolution Tools Available
The TBTF framework provides authorities with:
1. **Bail-in**: Write down or convert liabilities to equity
2. **Transfer**: Transfer critical functions to a bridge bank or third party
3. **Separation**: Separate systemically important functions from the rest
4. **Liquidation**: Orderly wind-down of non-systemic parts

## What Actually Happened (The Framework Was Bypassed)

The critical structural observation: **the formal TBTF resolution framework was NOT used**. Instead:

1. The Federal Council enacted an **Emergency Ordinance** (March 16-19, 2023) operating outside the normal TBTF framework
2. The merger was arranged through **ad hoc political negotiation**, not the formal resolution process
3. **FINMA used emergency ordinance powers** (Article 5a) rather than standard bail-in tools
4. The shareholder approval requirement was **overridden** by Article 10a of the emergency ordinance

This matters because the formal framework would have prescribed a different hierarchy of losses. Under normal bail-in, equity would have been wiped out before AT1 bonds. The emergency ordinance allowed the inversion.

## Why the Framework Was Bypassed

According to subsequent analysis:
- **Speed**: The crisis unfolded over a single weekend (March 18-19). The formal resolution process would take weeks or months.
- **Complexity**: CS's global operations made a formal resolution extremely complex — operations in dozens of jurisdictions, derivatives books, client relationships.
- **Confidence**: Authorities feared that initiating formal resolution would trigger further panic and contagion to UBS and other European banks.
- **Political**: A forced merger with UBS was politically preferable to a formal bank failure.

## Key Observations

The existence of the TBTF framework might suggest "orderly resolution under existing rules" was the most likely outcome. However, several factors pointed away from that:
1. The framework had never been tested on a G-SIB
2. The emergency ordinance allowed bypassing the framework
3. Political considerations can override legal frameworks

In practice, "orderly resolution" was less likely than "ad hoc political solution" — which is what actually happened.
