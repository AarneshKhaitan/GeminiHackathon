# Analyst and Commentator Attacks on Credit Suisse During Crisis Week

Not everyone was defending Credit Suisse during the week of March 13-17, 2023. A number of analysts and commentators were blunt about what they saw as a bank in terminal decline.

"Credit Suisse has been a walking dead bank for at least two years. The only question was when, not if." That was a common refrain. Others ticked through the litany: "This is a bank that lost $5.5 billion on Archegos, had $10 billion frozen in Greensill, lost 25% of its deposits in a single quarter, and is now caught in SVB contagion. How many more warnings do you need?" One analyst quipped, "CS has had more restructuring plans than successful quarters."

On governance and culture, the criticism was withering. "Three CEOs in three years. A chairman who resigned over a COVID quarantine breach. Spying on departing executives. This is not a well-run institution." The issue wasn't capital ratios or regulation, critics argued. "The problem is that nobody trusts CS's management to execute anything." As one commentator put it: "Every time CS says they've turned the corner, they walk into another wall."

The deposit flight numbers drew particular attention. CS had reported CHF 110 billion in deposit outflows in a single quarter. "You can't restructure your way out of that," said one banking analyst. Another argued the point more broadly: "Deposits are confidence. When a quarter of your deposit base leaves, confidence is gone. And confidence, once lost in banking, doesn't come back gradually — it comes back never." The Q4 2022 earnings call, said one observer, "showed a bank that is technically solvent but fundamentally broken."

On the SVB contagion angle, critics drew a distinction: "SVB didn't cause CS's problems. SVB exposed CS's problems. CS was already the weakest major bank in Europe." Or as one analyst colorfully put it: "If you're the sick animal in the herd, any loud noise will trigger the predators. SVB was the loud noise."

Credit default swap spreads became their own talking point. "CDS above 500bp for a G-SIB is not a warning sign. It's a death sentence. The market is telling you this bank is done." By March 15 with CDS above 800bp, one trader said: "When CDS hits 800bp, you're not pricing risk anymore. You're pricing certainty of government intervention."

Some analysts went further and predicted specific elements of the resolution. "If FINMA steps in, AT1 bondholders should be very worried. Swiss AT1 bonds have different write-down mechanics than EU bonds." Multiple commentators said the only realistic outcome was a forced merger with UBS — "Nothing else makes sense." One prescient voice noted: "Equity might actually survive this — the question is whether bondholders will."
