# SNB and FINMA Issue Joint Statement on Credit Suisse

Following the 30% crash in Credit Suisse shares on March 15, 2023, the Swiss National Bank and the Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority FINMA took the unusual step of issuing a joint public statement on the bank's condition.

The key line from the statement:

> "Credit Suisse meets the capital and liquidity requirements imposed on systemically important banks."

The SNB further stated it would provide Credit Suisse with liquidity "if necessary," signaling that emergency central bank facilities were available to the bank.

Both the central bank and the regulator were vouching specifically for CS's compliance with regulatory requirements — not a vague "we're monitoring the situation" but a direct claim that the bank met its capital and liquidity thresholds. CS's CET1 ratio stood at 14.1% versus an approximate 10% regulatory minimum.

Joint central bank-regulator statements of this kind are rare. The SNB does not issue public endorsements of banks meeting their requirements under normal circumstances.

For context, similar reassurances have been issued at other institutions in the past. SEC Chairman Chris Cox said Bear Stearns had "a strong capital position" on March 11, 2008; Bear was acquired by JPMorgan on March 16. Treasury Secretary Paulson expressed confidence in Lehman Brothers' capital in August 2008; Lehman filed for bankruptcy on September 15. The Bank of England said Northern Rock was "solvent" in September 2007; it was nationalized in February 2008.

In the days following the SNB/FINMA statement, events moved fast. On March 16, Credit Suisse drew CHF 50 billion from the SNB's emergency liquidity facility. On March 17, CS stock fell another 8% despite the liquidity draw. On March 19, UBS acquired Credit Suisse at CHF 0.76 per share, and AT1 bonds were written down to zero.

The joint statement bought Credit Suisse approximately 96 hours.

Sources: SNB Press Release (March 15, 2023); FINMA Communications (March 15, 2023).
