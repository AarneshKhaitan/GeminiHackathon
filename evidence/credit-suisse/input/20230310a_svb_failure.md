# Silicon Valley Bank collapse: How it happened

Source: CNBC, March 10, 2023 | FDIC | CNN

Wednesday March 8, 2023:
SVB announced it needed to raise $2.25 billion in capital to shore up its balance sheet. The bank had sold $21 billion in securities from its portfolio at a $1.8 billion loss. The sales were forced by declining deposits as tech startup clients burned through cash.

The announcement spooked investors and depositors. SVB stock began falling in after-hours trading.

Thursday March 9, 2023:
"The run on SVB deposits was stunning in its speed," according to later regulatory reports. Depositors attempted to withdraw approximately $42 billion in a single day — the fastest bank run in history.

VC firms including Peter Thiel's Founders Fund advised portfolio companies to pull deposits from SVB. Word spread through WhatsApp groups and Twitter. The digital bank run was amplified by social media in a way that no previous bank run had been.

SVB stock crashed 60% during the trading session. Trading was halted multiple times.

Friday March 10, 2023:
Before markets opened, the California Department of Financial Protection and Innovation closed SVB and appointed the FDIC as receiver. SVB had $209 billion in total assets and $175.4 billion in deposits at the time of failure.

It was the third-largest bank failure in US history (after Washington Mutual in 2008 and Continental Illinois in 1984 — later surpassed by First Republic in May 2023).

Sunday March 12, 2023:
Signature Bank (New York) also closed by regulators.

Federal government announced extraordinary measures:
- All SVB depositors (including uninsured deposits above $250K) would be made whole
- Federal Reserve created the Bank Term Funding Program (BTFP), allowing banks to borrow against securities at par value rather than market value
- Joint statement from Treasury, Fed, and FDIC: "The U.S. banking system remains resilient and on a solid foundation"

Root cause:
SVB had invested heavily in long-duration bonds and mortgage-backed securities during the 2020-2021 deposit boom. When the Fed raised rates aggressively (0.25% to 4.75% in 12 months), the market value of these holdings dropped dramatically. Unrealized losses exceeded SVB's equity. When depositors withdrew funds faster than SVB could liquidate assets, the bank was done.

Nearly half of US venture capital-backed healthcare and technology companies banked with SVB. It was a concentrated client base that moved as a herd.

Why this matters for CS: SVB's failure had nothing to do with Credit Suisse operationally. Different country, different business model, different problems. But it reignited global banking contagion fears. European bank stocks sold off across the board. And CS — already the weakest major European bank — got hit hardest.
