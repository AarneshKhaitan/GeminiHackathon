# Credit Suisse suspends $10 billion of funds linked to Greensill Capital

Reuters / Financial Times, March 2021

Credit Suisse has frozen approximately $10 billion in supply chain finance funds connected to Greensill Capital after the specialty finance firm lost its insurance coverage and collapsed into insolvency.

The four funds, marketed under the Credit Suisse Supply Chain Finance Funds banner, invested in notes originated by Greensill Capital, which provided short-term loans to companies based on expected future invoices. Greensill's insurance provider, a subsidiary of Tokio Marine, declined to renew coverage, triggering the firm's rapid unraveling.

Credit Suisse said it had suspended redemptions from the funds "to protect the interests of investors" and would begin liquidating the portfolios. The bank said it was working to recover as much value as possible for investors.

The funds had been marketed to Credit Suisse's wealth management clients as low-risk investments offering stable returns above money market rates. Some clients invested substantial portions of their portfolios in the products.

Lex Greensill, the firm's founder, had maintained close personal relationships with Credit Suisse personnel. Questions have been raised about whether the bank conducted adequate due diligence on Greensill's lending practices, which in some cases involved advancing money against prospective future invoices rather than existing receivables.

The Greensill collapse came in the same month as the Archegos Capital Management debacle, which cost Credit Suisse approximately $5.5 billion. Together, the two events represented over $15 billion in losses or frozen assets in a single month, prompting a comprehensive review of the bank's risk management practices.

Credit Suisse Chairman António Horta-Osório, who was brought in to reform the bank after these twin scandals, himself resigned in January 2022 after an internal investigation found he had violated COVID-19 quarantine rules on multiple occasions.

The Greensill affair led to multiple lawsuits from investors in the funds, ongoing regulatory investigations, and further erosion of Credit Suisse's already damaged reputation.
