# Credit Suisse AT1 Bonds Collapse to Zero in Crisis Week

Credit Suisse's Additional Tier 1 bonds, already trading at distressed levels before the March 2023 crisis, collapsed through the floor over the course of a single week. By the time FINMA ordered a full write-down on March 19, the market had been pricing catastrophic losses for days.

The trajectory, in approximate terms:

| Date | Approx. AT1 Price (cents/dollar) | Context |
|------|----------------------------------|---------|
| Early March | ~60-70 | Already distressed |
| March 13 | ~50-55 | Post-SVB contagion |
| March 14 | ~45-50 | Continued selling |
| March 15 AM | ~40-45 | Pre-Saudi interview |
| March 15 PM | ~20-30 | Post-Saudi interview crash |
| March 16 | ~25-35 | After SNB facility announcement, partial bounce |
| March 17 | ~15-25 | Market doubts sustainability |
| March 19 (announced) | 0 | Full write-down ordered by FINMA |

Credit Suisse had 13 AT1 instruments outstanding, issued between 2013 and 2022, with a total face value of approximately CHF 16 billion (roughly $17.3 billion). All of it was written down to zero. The bonds were primarily held by institutional investors, pension funds, and fixed-income funds.

By March 17, with AT1s trading at 15-25 cents on the dollar, the market was already pricing in a high probability of severe losses. The full write-down to zero on March 19 was not entirely a surprise — but the hierarchy inversion was. This was the first time AT1 bonds at a major bank were written down to zero while equity retained value. UBS paid CHF 0.76 per share for Credit Suisse, meaning shareholders got something while bondholders nominally senior to them got nothing. Previous AT1 write-downs, such as at Banco Popular in 2017, had followed the normal creditor hierarchy with equity wiped out first.

The fallout was immediate and global. On March 20, AT1 bonds at other banks dropped 5-15% as the roughly $275 billion global AT1 market repriced. Investors scrambled to reassess the structural risk embedded in these instruments. The ECB and Bank of England both issued statements clarifying that in their jurisdictions, AT1 bonds would follow the normal creditor hierarchy — equity would absorb losses before bondholders.

Sources: Reuters AT1 bond price tracking (March 2023); Bloomberg Credit Suisse AT1 bond market data; various fixed-income analyst reports.
