# 'FTX Has Been Hacked': Crypto Disaster Worsens as Exchange Sees Mysterious Outflows Exceeding $600M

By Cheyenne Ligon, Sam Reynolds, Sam Kessler, Nikhilesh De | Edited by Nick Baker
Published Nov 12, 2022, 4:20 a.m. | Updated Jan 10, 2025, 7:51 p.m.

Over $600 million vanished from FTX's crypto wallets hours after the exchange filed for bankruptcy on November 11, 2022. Company officials confirmed the incident through their official Telegram channel, warning users about potential security threats.

An account administrator in the FTX Support Telegram chat posted: "FTX has been hacked. FTX apps are malware. Delete them." The message was pinned by FTX General Counsel Ryne Miller, instructing users to avoid the FTX website as it "might download Trojans."

Hours after the incident, Miller disclosed on Twitter that "FTX US and FTX.com had been moving all their digital assets to cold storage" because of Friday's bankruptcy filing. He noted the process was "expedited this evening – to mitigate damage upon observing unauthorized transactions."

Many FTX wallet holders reported zero balances in their accounts. Various Ethereum tokens, Solana, and Binance Smart Chain tokens exited FTX's official wallets and moved to decentralized exchanges like 1inch. Both FTX and FTX US appeared affected.

Speculation circulated online about whether the outflows represented an external attack or an inside job. Twitter analyst ZachXBT reported that "multiple former FTX employees confirmed to me that they do not recognize these transfers," suggesting potential insider involvement.

Around midnight Eastern time, FTX's login portal became unavailable, displaying a 503 error when users attempted access — typically indicating server unavailability or maintenance.

The Securities Commission of the Bahamas later confirmed on November 18 that it had directed the transfer of FTX digital assets to government-controlled wallets for "safekeeping." The commission said it "exercised its powers as a regulator to protect the interests of clients and creditors of FTX." However, this only accounted for a portion of the total movements.

Blockchain analytics firms including Elliptic and Chainalysis tracked the movements across multiple chains. The draining address was labeled by Etherscan as "FTX Accounts Drainer." Some tokens were swapped through decentralized exchanges — a pattern consistent with attempted laundering — while others were simply moved to new wallets.

The full accounting of which movements were authorized regulatory seizures versus unauthorized theft took months to sort out during the bankruptcy proceedings.
