# Bahamas Regulatory Framework — Securities Commission of The Bahamas (SCB)

## The DARE Act (2020)

The Digital Assets and Registered Exchanges (DARE) Act was enacted in 2020 by the Bahamas. It was one of the world's first comprehensive frameworks to regulate digital assets, covering:

- Issuing and trading of crypto tokens
- Oversight of exchanges (both spot and derivatives)
- Oversight of wallet and custody services
- Licensing for digital asset service providers

FTX Digital Markets Ltd. (FTX's Bahamian entity) was registered and regulated under this framework. This was a key part of FTX's legitimacy narrative — "we're one of the few exchanges that's actually regulated."

## Critical Weakness: No Customer Fund Segregation Requirement

The DARE Act was **superficial in most matters and had many weak points**. Most critically:

- **Did NOT require segregation of customer funds** from the exchange's own funds
- Did not mandate regular independent audits of customer balances
- Had limited capacity for ongoing supervision of large, complex entities like FTX
- Enforcement mechanisms were underdeveloped

This is the structural gap that enabled the fraud. In traditional finance (e.g., US broker-dealer regulation under SEC Rule 15c3-3), customer funds MUST be segregated. In the Bahamas, there was no such requirement in the regulatory framework itself — FTX's promise to segregate funds was contractual (in its ToS), not regulatory.

## SCB's Capacity and Oversight

The Securities Commission of the Bahamas was a small regulatory body overseeing a rapidly growing crypto industry:
- Limited staff and technical expertise for supervising complex crypto operations
- No real-time monitoring of on-chain flows
- Relied heavily on self-reporting by regulated entities
- Did not have the resources to audit a multi-billion-dollar global operation

## SCB Actions During the Crisis (November 2022)

When FTX collapsed:

1. **November 10**: SCB froze FTX Digital Markets' registration
2. **November 12**: SCB ordered FTX crypto to be transferred to government-controlled wallets
3. SCB ultimately took custody of FTX deposits valued at over **$3.5 billion**
4. This asset seizure led to jurisdictional disputes with US bankruptcy proceedings

## The Jurisdictional Arbitrage

FTX's choice to base in the Bahamas rather than the US was itself a structural decision:
- US regulation (SEC, CFTC) would have imposed stricter requirements
- The Bahamas offered a lighter regulatory touch with the legitimacy of being "regulated"
- SBF publicly touted regulatory engagement while benefiting from regulatory gaps
- This is a form of "regulatory arbitrage" — choosing the jurisdiction with the most favorable (i.e., lightest) rules

## Key Observations

1. "FTX is regulated" — TRUE, but the regulatory framework had critical gaps
2. "Regulation means customer funds are protected" — FALSE in this jurisdiction
3. "Bahamian regulation is equivalent to US regulation" — FALSE, fundamentally different oversight capacity
4. The regulatory framework defined the space of possible interventions — in the Bahamas, there were limited tools to prevent or quickly address commingling

## Post-FTX Reforms

In 2023, the Bahamas proposed significantly tighter crypto regulations:
- New requirements for stablecoins
- Enhanced oversight of exchanges
- Measures described as potentially "among the most advanced pieces of digital-asset legislation in the world"
- In 2024, a new comprehensive Digital Assets Act was passed

The fact that reforms were needed confirms the pre-crisis framework's inadequacy.
